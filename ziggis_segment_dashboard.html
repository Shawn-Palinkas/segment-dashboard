📋 Network summary · 117 active locations · 2026 YTD
Performance is split with a weak majority. Of 117 active stores, 26 are strong ($3,000+/day), 37 medium ($2,400–$2,999), and 54 weak (under $2,400). Nearly half the network is underperforming, which means segment profiling is a practical tool — not just an analytical exercise — for understanding what separates successful markets from struggling ones.

Dog Lovers and Outdoor Adventures are the clearest success signals. Dog Lovers appears in 23% of strong stores but only 4% of weak ones — a +19 gap that no other segment matches. Outdoor Adventures is close behind at +16. Stores with both segments in their top 5 are the most reliable performers in the network.

Praise & Worship is the strongest warning sign. It appears in 0% of strong stores and 19% of weak ones — a −19 gap, the worst in the network. Pieces of History (−13), Gratitude (−11), and Artistic Appreciation (−11) follow. Locations with two or more of these four segments in their top 5 account for a disproportionate share of the weak tier and represent the highest closure risk in the active network.

Colorado is the core, but the newer markets are diversifying fast. Colorado alone accounts for 61 of 117 stores. The 23 locations opened since 2025 span 15 new states and are performing at a wide range — from Beaumont CA at $4,960/day (strong) to Derby KS at $2,045/day (weak). That spread reflects both the opportunity and the risk of rapid geographic expansion without a consistent segment profile in target markets. The Signal Analysis tab identifies which new locations have the customer foundation to grow and which may need early intervention.

🔒 5 closed locations
📋 Key patterns across 5 closed locations
Sales floors were far below viability at all 5 locations. Every closed store averaged under $2,200/day over its lifetime — well below the $2,400 weak-tier threshold. The Amarillo locations never cleared $1,550/day, Tulsa and Fort Collins hovered around $1,160–$1,185/day despite years of operation, and even the best performer (Katy at $2,117/day) was trending downward with no segment-based reason to expect recovery.

The Fort Collins anomaly is the most instructive case. Fort Collins 1880 N College Ave is the only closed store with genuinely strong segment signals — Yoga Advocates (#1), Outdoor Adventures (#2), Hops & Brews (#8), and Farm Culture (#4) are all present. By segment profile alone it resembles an active medium-to-strong store. Yet it averaged $1,184/day for over three years and was essentially flat from 2024 to 2025. This is a clear signal that strong segments are necessary but not sufficient — location-specific factors (site visibility, traffic patterns, competition, execution) can override a favorable customer profile entirely.

Weak-market patterns dominated the Texas closures. Praise & Worship (−19 gap, the single worst signal in the network) was the top segment at Katy. Pieces of History (−13 gap) was the top segment at Amarillo Hillside. Tulsa showed Pieces of History in its top 5 as well. None of the Texas or Oklahoma stores had a single strong-market signal in their top 5. Combined with the 2 currently active Texas stores (Richmond at ~$705/day and Sulphur Springs at ~$1,348/day) both sitting in the weak tier, the data makes a consistent case that Texas and Oklahoma trade areas are producing segment profiles that do not align with Ziggi's strongest customer base.

Sales trajectories gave advance warning. Amarillo Hillside declined 17% from 2024 to 2025. Katy declined 8%. Fort Collins was flat for three consecutive years with no upward movement. Only Tulsa showed a YoY increase (+25%), but even its improved 2025 average of $1,288/day was still less than half the strong-tier threshold. In each case, the sales data and segment profile together pointed toward closure well before it happened.
